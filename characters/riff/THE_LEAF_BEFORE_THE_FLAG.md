# The Leaf Before the Flag

**Format:** Origin vignette treatment  
**Canon status:** Approved foundation; prose details remain flexible  
**Purpose:** Establish Riff's early connection to Canada without choosing his
birthplace or exact immigration timeline

## Logline

Years after a childhood spent admiring a beautiful red leaf and a fiercely
precise wrestler, Riff discovers that both belonged to the country he now calls
home.

## Story treatment

### 1. The late broadcast

Riff is young and living somewhere outside Canada. The place is deliberately
unidentified.

On certain nights, he is allowed to stay awake for wrestling. The television
makes most colours look tired, but pink still cuts through the room.

Bret Hart enters.

Riff does not understand nationality, reputation, or the larger mythology. He
understands that Bret looks prepared. Every movement seems to have a reason.
While everyone around him reacts to the spectacle, Riff watches the precision.

He begins practising entrance poses in reflective windows and taking tiny daily
tasks much too seriously.

### 2. The shape

Around the same period, Riff encounters a red maple leaf. Where he sees it is
left open: it might be on a broadcast graphic, a label, a piece of clothing, or
a page.

He does not know that it belongs to a flag.

He likes that the shape feels natural and designed at the same time. He draws it
badly in the margin of his map: too many points on one side, not enough on the
other. He keeps drawing it anyway.

### 3. Time passes

The story avoids a montage of immigration paperwork or a single dramatic
arrival. Riff's move to Canada is larger than one image, and its details are not
yet canon.

Instead, we meet him later, already learning how to live in his new home.

He knows some routes and not others. He knows which doors stick in winter. He
knows how long “just down the street” can mean. He still checks the map even
when pretending he does not need it.

### 4. Recognition

One day, Riff looks up and sees the Canadian flag moving above an ordinary
place—a community centre, transit stop, school, or neighbourhood building.

He stops.

It is the leaf.

Not a similar leaf. The leaf.

The discovery is funny before it is emotional. Riff is briefly offended that
nobody informed him the shape had been a whole country this entire time.

Then he realizes that something from his childhood has arrived here with him.

### 5. The second discovery

Later, someone mentions that Bret Hart is from Calgary.

Riff assumes they are joking.

When he confirms it, he sits quietly with the coincidence: the performer who
taught him that precision could be heroic and the symbol he drew before he
understood flags both came from Canada.

Canada had been present in his imagination before it became his address.

### 6. The patch

Riff makes a small maple-leaf patch himself. It is visibly imperfect and more
meaningful because of it.

He sews it onto his explorer satchel beside the star that marks a new side
quest.

The star points forward.

The leaf reminds him that parts of home may have been travelling beside him for
longer than he knew.

## Closing image

Riff stands at a Toronto transit stop after midnight, map open, leaf patch
visible, watching snow or rain catch the coloured light from somewhere nearby.

The vehicle he needs passes in the opposite direction.

Riff looks at the map, looks at the disappearing lights, and says:

> “Okay. That was not on the map.”

He starts walking.

## Thematic line

Sometimes you belong to a place in pieces before you know its name.
