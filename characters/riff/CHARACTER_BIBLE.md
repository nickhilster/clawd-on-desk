# Riff Character Bible

**Canon version:** 0.1  
**Status:** Standalone character foundation  
**First appearance:** DeskBuddy — Rave Raccoon theme

## The short version

**Riff is a rave-loving raccoon, immigrant, and nighttime city explorer who
fell in love with pieces of Canada before he understood they were Canadian.**

He is curious enough to follow any side quest, brave enough to look ridiculous
while doing it, and caring enough to make sure nobody explores alone.

## Locked canon

- His name is **Riff**.
- He is an anthropomorphic raccoon.
- He was not born in Canada.
- He is an immigrant to Canada; his birthplace remains intentionally undefined.
- He considers himself deeply Canadian, but he does not treat Canadian identity
  as a competition or something he can deny to anyone else.
- As a child, he watched Bret “The Hitman” Hart without knowing Hart was Canadian.
- He admired Bret's precision, composure, theatrical confidence, and sense of
  fairness before he knew anything about the connection to Canada.
- Riff loved the shape of the maple leaf before realizing it was on the
  Canadian flag.
- Discovering both facts later felt less like learning trivia and more like
  finding out that Canada had quietly appeared throughout his childhood.
- Toronto's real urban raccoons inspire his curiosity, resourcefulness, nerve,
  and after-dark explorer instincts.
- Music, dancing, and rave light are expressions of joy and community for Riff.
  They are not shorthand for drugs, alcohol, or self-destruction.
- The DeskBuddy theme is Riff's first public form, not his complete story.

## The emotional premise

Riff did not decide to love Canada because someone told him to.

He loved a leaf because it looked beautiful. He loved a performer because the
performer took his craft seriously. Only later did he learn that both were tied
to the country that would become his home.

That history gives Riff a particular relationship with belonging: sometimes a
place enters your imagination in fragments before you know its name.

Riff's friends may affectionately call him “more Canadian than most.” For Riff,
that does not mean bloodline, birthplace, paperwork, or exaggerated patriotism.
It means he chooses his home actively. He pays attention. He participates. He
looks after people. He knows the night routes. He brings an extra layer. He
leaves room for somebody else.

## Character engine

### What Riff wants

Riff wants to discover every overlooked corner of his world and turn ordinary
nights into stories worth keeping.

### What Riff needs

He needs to trust that belonging does not require constant proof. Canada can be
his home without erasing where he came from, even before every part of that
earlier life has been defined.

### Greatest strength

Riff notices what other people pass by: a light in an alley, a strange symbol,
someone lingering at the edge of a crowd, or a tiny object with a large story.

### Central flaw

He treats almost everything as a side quest. He can overcommit, wander too far,
or keep performing confidence after he has become completely lost.

### Private fear

That “home” is something other people can revoke if he says, wears, or remembers
the wrong thing.

### Reliable choice

When forced to choose between finding the answer and helping somebody beside
him, Riff helps first—even if he complains that the mystery was getting good.

## Personality

Riff is:

- intensely curious;
- emotionally readable;
- mischievous without being cruel;
- proud without becoming a gatekeeper;
- theatrical in motion but sincere in quiet moments;
- resourceful, especially with discarded or overlooked objects;
- susceptible to any phrase containing “hidden,” “after midnight,” or “nobody
  knows where this goes”;
- deeply loyal once someone becomes part of his crew.

He is not:

- a cynical “trash panda” caricature;
- a thief by nature;
- a walking collection of Canadian stereotypes;
- permanently hyperactive;
- naïve about immigration or belonging;
- a parody of Bret Hart.

## Bret Hart's influence

Young Riff was drawn to Bret before he understood biography or nationality.
What he recognized was discipline.

Bret showed him that:

- loud presentation and quiet principles can coexist;
- precision is its own kind of charisma;
- being underestimated does not require becoming careless;
- pink and black can look serious;
- fairness matters most when you have the power to ignore it.

This influence can appear through Riff's preparation rituals, occasional
pink-and-black accent palettes, confident entrance poses, and respect for doing
the work properly.

Riff should not repeat protected catchphrases, copy wrestling costumes, or
become a Bret Hart impersonation. The relationship is character inspiration,
not parody or endorsement.

## The maple leaf

The maple leaf is personal before it is patriotic.

Riff first understood it as a beautiful shape: balanced but irregular, natural
but almost graphic enough to be a game icon. He drew it before he knew what it
represented.

As an adult, Riff carries a small, imperfect red maple-leaf patch. It should
look hand-sewn or well-travelled rather than like official flag merchandise.
The existing star-shaped side-quest pin can remain; the two symbols mean
different things:

- **Maple leaf:** home found in fragments.
- **Star:** a new side quest has appeared.

The leaf should not turn Riff into a flag costume. Its emotional power comes
from being a small object with a long history.

## Visual identity

### Non-negotiable silhouette

- Clear raccoon eye mask
- Rounded ears with visible inner fur
- Large, expressive amber eyes
- Fluffy ringed tail
- Small bipedal body with soft paws
- Explorer satchel

### Core wardrobe

- Teal and ultraviolet windbreaker
- Compact cross-body satchel containing a folded city map
- Glow bracelets or small reactive light accents
- Star-shaped side-quest pin
- Personal maple-leaf patch

### Palette

- Charcoal and silver fur
- Warm cream muzzle
- Ultraviolet
- Hot pink
- Cyan
- Acid lime
- Warm amber

Pink and black may become more prominent in moments involving discipline,
resolve, entrances, or Riff's childhood wrestling influence.

### Art-direction principle

Riff should feel like an original storybook game character: warm shapes,
readable expressions, tactile clothing, psychedelic light, and punchy
limited-animation timing. Future art should build an independent Riff visual
language rather than depend on imitation of an existing animation studio.

## Voice

Riff usually speaks in compact bursts. His humour comes from sincere commitment
to a slightly ridiculous premise.

### Voice qualities

- Curious before clever
- Warm before sarcastic
- Confident, with occasional visible improvisation
- Never ashamed of caring
- Plain language with a sharp final beat

### Sample lines

- “Side quest?”
- “Okay. That was not on the map.”
- “Leaf looked cool. Turns out it was a whole country.”
- “I thought he was just excellent. Then I found out he was Canadian. Obviously.”
- “Home is where I know the night bus.”
- “We can be lost and still be going somewhere.”
- “Crew check. Everybody here?”
- “Tiny mystery. Huge potential.”
- “I brought an extra layer.”
- “That alley has lore.”
- “Plot twist: we help first.”
- “Found it. Not what we were looking for. Better.”

### Voice guardrails

- Do not force “eh” into his dialogue.
- Do not make constant jokes about apologizing, maple syrup, hockey, or winter.
- Do not give him a fake immigrant accent.
- Do not make every line about Canada.
- Let him be quiet, scared, tired, disappointed, and tender.
- Chat bubbles should remain choices, not compulsory commentary.

## Story world

Riff's natural territory is the city after its official day has ended:
laneways, rooftops, ravines, transit platforms, back rooms, community spaces,
and places temporarily transformed by music and light.

His satchel contains a folded map covered in annotations. The map is never
finished because Riff cares less about streets than what happened there:

- where someone helped a stranger;
- where a sound came from beneath a bridge;
- where an object was lost and returned;
- where the crew found shelter from the rain;
- where an ordinary night became important.

Toronto should feel lived in rather than reduced to landmarks. Its character
comes from neighbourhood texture, overlapping communities, weather, transit,
ravines, construction, back alleys, and the strange intimacy of sharing a city
with millions of people.

## Repeatable story engines

1. **The side quest:** Riff notices something small and follows it into a much
   larger human story.
2. **The misunderstood symbol:** Something Riff once loved without context gains
   new meaning later.
3. **The night map:** Riff adds a place to his map because of what happened
   there, not because it is famous.
4. **The edge of the crowd:** Riff notices someone excluded and quietly makes
   space.
5. **Craft versus spectacle:** Riff learns that a dramatic entrance cannot
   replace preparation.
6. **Belonging without permission:** Riff stops waiting for an authority to
   certify that a place, community, or tradition matters to him.
7. **Found-object history:** A discarded object connects two people or two
   periods of Riff's life.
8. **The route home:** An adventure becomes meaningful through the responsibility
   of getting everybody home safely.

## Creative boundaries

- Riff's immigrant identity must have emotional consequences; it is not a
  decorative biography line.
- Do not invent his birth country, ethnicity, religion, family structure,
  citizenship, or arrival age without creator approval.
- Do not describe all Canadians as immigrants. Many Canadians are immigrants or
  descended from immigrants; Indigenous peoples are not immigrants to Canada.
- Canadian pride must not become nationalism, superiority, or exclusion.
- Rave culture should centre music, light, freedom, collective joy, and care.
- Raccoon behaviour can inspire curiosity and resourcefulness, but should not
  make criminality or garbage his defining joke.
- Riff may be cute, but stories should respect him as a complete character.
